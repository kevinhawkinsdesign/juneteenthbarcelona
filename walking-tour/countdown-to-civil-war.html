Countdown to Civil War

StartSlave TradePre-WarCivil WarEl RavalPost-War

The following timeline traces the political upheavals in Spain that set the stage for the Civil War — a conflict that drew Black American intellectuals and volunteers to Barcelona in remarkable numbers.

1898
El Desastre — Loss of the Colonies

The Spanish navy was badly defeated by the US in a war over its last major imperial colonies: Cuba, Puerto Rico, and the Philippines. The humiliating loss — known as el Desastre — exposed the deep economic, social, and military weakness of the country. Spanish intellectuals called for a national 'regeneration.'

Spain also faced domestic rebellions in its protectorate in Morocco and growing pressure around military conscription. Because wealthy families could pay to exempt their sons, it was poor workers and peasants who filled the ranks — sent to fight in miserable conditions far from home.

1909
Semana Trágica — The Tragic Week

During a week of violent unrest in Barcelona, Socialists and Republicans called a general strike to protest the massacre of Spanish soldiers in Morocco. When the strike turned violent, churches and convents were set ablaze. The government responded with brutal military force — executing dozens and sending hundreds more to prison.

1921
Battle of Annual — Rif Massacre

In one of Spain's worst military disasters, 15,000 Spanish soldiers were killed by Rif Berber fighters in northern Morocco. The catastrophic defeat deepened public outrage over the colonial war and the governing class's handling of it — adding further fuel to the political crisis tearing the country apart.

1923
Primo de Rivera's Coup

To prevent further political destabilisation, General Miguel Primo de Rivera staged a coup with the blessing of King Alfonso XIII. He installed what he called a 'soft' dictatorship — suspending parliament and censoring the press — and ruled until 1930. His regime failed to stop the country from pulling further apart along left and right-wing lines.

1926
Morocco — Brutal Suppression

The Rif Rebellion was finally crushed — with extreme violence, including the use of mustard gas against civilian populations. Acts of torture and mutilation were widespread on both sides.

The Moroccan campaigns became a training ground for a generation of Spanish officers, among them Francisco Franco. The tactics rehearsed in North Africa would later be unleashed against Spain's own people during the Civil War.

1931
The Second Republic Declared

Republican parties swept local elections across Spain's major cities. The result was widely read as a rejection of the monarchy — and within days, King Alfonso XIII fled the country. The Second Republic was proclaimed on April 14, 1931.

The Republic raised expectations of genuine reform: better conditions for workers, land redistribution for peasants, and regional autonomy for Catalonia, the Basque Country, and Galicia. But it was seen as a direct threat by landowners, industrialists, the Church, and senior military officers — setting the stage for a violent backlash.

1936
The Civil War Begins

A left-wing coalition — the Frente Popular (Popular Front), uniting Republicans, Socialists, Communists, and the Anarchist union CNT — won the February elections. The new government restored Catalan autonomy, extended rights to the Basque Country, and pushed forward a more radical programme of agrarian reform.

The right could not accept it. Landowners, the Church, and key military figures began plotting a coup. On July 17, the Spanish Army of Africa rose up in Morocco, with simultaneous uprisings across mainland Spain. The country split in two: the elected Republican government on one side, and Franco's Nationalist forces on the other.

The Spanish Civil War had begun — and the world was watching.
